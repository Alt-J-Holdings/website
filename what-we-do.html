How we close the distance.

Closing the distance between where a business is and what it could achieve means doing the work.
There are no shortcuts.

The Firm

Alt+J Partners is a private investment firm and multi-family office focused on the lower middle market. We partner with founder-owned, family-owned, and management-led businesses at inflection points — bringing patient capital, operating experience, and the structural advantages of family office capital.

We pursue a flexible mandate including control recapitalizations, founder transitions, corporate carve-outs, special situations, and technology-enabled transformations of established lower middle market businesses. The firm invests in companies with $20 million to $100 million in revenue and $4 million to $15 million in EBITDA across services, consumer, and traditional industry sectors.

The Insight

Capital alone has never been the difference.

The companies we invest in don't need a check and a quarterly call. They need a partner who has built and operated businesses — and who understands that the real work happens between the milestones.

01

Understand

We start by listening. Before strategy, before a thesis, before any capital discussion — we learn the business from the inside. Its history, its people, its inflection point, and the specific distance between where it is and what it could become.

02

Build Together

We work alongside leadership — not above them. From financial infrastructure to operating cadence to talent strategy, we bring the frameworks and the experience to help a company level up systematically, one layer at a time.

03

Stay

The hardest part of building a great company isn't the first year — it's years three through ten. We stay through every layer of the journey, from stabilization through category leadership. No fund clock. No forced exit. We leave when the work is done.

This process isn't theoretical. It's the operating system we've built across every company in our portfolio — and it maps directly to a framework we call the Alt+J Hierarchy of Needs.

The Framework

The Alt+J Hierarchy of Needs

Setting the Standard · Delta Organization

This is the rarest layer, and the one most worth reaching. A company at this peak has become the reference point in its category — the one competitors study, the one customers prefer, the one talent wants to join. From the boardroom to the frontline, everyone knows what the company is for and how their work connects to it. The strategy works. The systems hold. The culture compounds. The numbers prove it. The business has become more than the sum of any individual leader, founder, or moment — it’s a Delta Organization, capable of producing transformational performance on a sustained basis.

Most capital partners will never help a company reach this layer because their fund structures will not let them stay long enough. Alt+J was built specifically to. As a family office, we have the time, the alignment, and the operating experience to support a company all the way through — past the easy wins, past the obvious optimizations, into the harder and longer work of becoming category-defining. This is the level we measure ourselves by. Not just the return we generated, but the company we left behind.

Aiming Higher · Ambition

Plenty of companies operate. Fewer reach. At this layer, the question shifts from are we running well to are we trying to be more than we are today. Real goals. Not aspirational language in a deck — concrete commitments with plans behind them, owned by specific people, tracked on a real cadence. Most lower middle market companies stall here. They have ambition in the founder’s head but no system for translating it into the business. The company keeps doing what it knows how to do, and the gap between current performance and full potential quietly widens.

We have set ambitious goals in our own businesses, missed some of them, hit others, and learned what makes the difference. That experience translates directly into what we bring at this layer: the operating frameworks that turn ambition into execution — OKRs, EOS, balanced scorecards, whichever fits the business — and the discipline to make those frameworks stick rather than fade after the first quarterly review. We come in with opinions about what real ambition looks like, and we are willing to share them. The peak of the hierarchy isn’t reachable without this layer working.

Work That Matters · Belonging

This is the layer most companies underestimate. Once the business is running cleanly, the question becomes whether it’s a place worth being. Whether the people doing the work understand why the work matters. Whether top performers stay or quietly start looking. Whether the company has a story big enough to draw great people in and keep them, or whether it’s just a job — fine compensation, competent management, no real reason to be there over anywhere else. Most lower middle market companies have a culture by accident. The ones that pull away have one by design.

The companies we have built that worked best were the ones where the team knew exactly what they were part of. That insight shapes how we engage at this layer. We help articulate the narrative — what the company is for, where it’s going, why anyone should care — and we help build the operating practices that make that narrative real day-to-day: how decisions get made, how recognition works, how leadership communicates when things are hard. A culture with a story behind it shows up everywhere — in retention, in customer experience, in how hard people work when it counts. We have seen that difference from the inside, and we know what it takes to build it.

Out of the Weeds · Safety

The business runs, but leadership is buried in it. The CEO is reviewing accounts payable, fielding HR escalations, putting out fires that should never have reached their desk. Every week starts with a calendar full of operational firefighting and ends with the strategic work undone. The business is functional, but it’s functional because of leadership’s heroics — and heroics don’t scale. There’s a quiet awareness that if leadership took a real vacation, things would slow down or break. The company runs on people, not on systems.

We have run companies where we were the bottleneck, and we have built the systems that fixed it. That’s what we bring at this layer: financial reporting infrastructure that produces signal instead of noise, governance practices that scale beyond a single decision-maker, operating cadences that keep the right work moving without leadership in every meeting. The goal isn’t sophistication for its own sake. It’s giving leadership their time back so they can focus on the questions only they can answer — and trusting the business to keep running while they do.

Keeping the Lights On · Survival

Every business owner knows this layer, even when the numbers say everything is fine. It’s the Sunday-night check of the bank balance. The quarter where one big customer is slow to pay and suddenly payroll is a question. The pit-of-the-stomach awareness that a single concentrated client loss, an unexpected covenant breach, or one bad month could put the whole thing at risk. Most lower middle market companies live closer to this edge than they admit out loud — even profitable ones. Surviving isn’t binary. It’s a daily question that the owner alone tends to carry.

We start here because we have lived it. Before Alt+J, we built companies, ran them, and learned exactly how lonely the bottom of the hierarchy is. That experience shapes what we bring to this layer: capital that arrives without strings and stays without a clock, the structural work to address concentration risk and banking relationships, and the perspective of partners who know that strategy doesn’t matter if the company can’t reliably exist next quarter. The base has to be solid. We start here because we know exactly why.

In Practice

What working with Alt+J looks like.

We join your board. We have real opinions and we share them. We are present in moments that matter and reachable in moments that do not. We bring resources when we have them, honesty when we do not. We are on your side — which sometimes means we push back.

We are not managing a relationship from a quarterly distance. We are doing the work.

What We Avoid

We are not the right fit for everyone.

  • Businesses where financial engineering is the primary value creation strategy.
  • Situations where culture, leadership, or customer relationships are fundamentally broken.
  • Commodity businesses with no clear path to differentiation.
  • Situations that need a financial sponsor. We are a partner, not a sponsor.
